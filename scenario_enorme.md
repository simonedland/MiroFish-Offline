The 1,000 SMS Bots

A synthetic SMS world made up of 1,000 distinct agents. Most are ordinary people with normal rhythms, habits, and relationships. Their messages form the background noise of everyday life: school plans, work updates, family logistics, parcels, gym sessions, football opinions, travel delays, and late-night gaming.

Underneath that, there is a smaller but noticeable layer of coordinated, higher-risk traffic. On the surface it blends in. In pattern, it does not.

Normal People (900 agents)

These 900 agents create the believable human baseline. Their behavior is repetitive in a natural way: routines, repeated contacts, time-of-day habits, short-term bursts, long quiet periods, and little social clusters that overlap.

The Teenagers (100 agents)

Sixteen to nineteen years old. Fast, emotional, chaotic, social. Their messages come in bursts, especially after school, in the evenings, and late on weekends. They use slang, abbreviations, emojis, inside jokes, and unfinished thoughts. Plans change constantly. A large share of their messages are about almost nothing, which is exactly what makes them feel real.

Within this group are recognizable subtypes:

the always-late one who changes plans three times in ten minutes

the organizer who acts like a secretary for the friend group

the one-word replier who somehow remains socially central

the oversharer who sends essays at midnight

the exam-disappearing student who vanishes for a week

the gamer-teen who only wakes up fully when talking about patches or clips

the lurker who rarely initiates but always reacts

the meme-only contact

the low-drama reliable one everyone texts for practical answers

the friendship-chaos magnet who is always in the middle of some issue

As a population, they go quiet during lessons, spike between 15:00 and 01:00, and create dense, overlapping social chatter.

The Office Workers (100 agents)

Professionals in their late twenties to fifties. Their SMS behavior is constrained by calendars, meetings, and the habit of being “brief but polite.” Activity clusters around commuting hours, lunch, and the workday itself. Their language is cleaner, more structured, and more predictable than the teenagers’.

Common types include:

mid-level managers who text in action items

junior employees who over-confirm everything

remote workers who seem to live in the wrong timezone

the office gossip who knows everything before HR does

the almost-quitting employee who vents twice a week

after-work social planners

deadline people who become briefly frantic at quarter-end

support staff coordinating rooms, access, and timing

sales people sending “running 10 min late” texts constantly

founders or leads who send short messages at odd hours

They are most active from 07:30 to 18:00, with a sharp drop after work except for the chronically overworked.

The Parents (100 agents)

Adults using SMS primarily as infrastructure. Their messages are practical, compressed, and tied to the movement of children, appointments, school obligations, errands, and home life. Their contacts are narrow and repetitive: partner, children, family members, one or two other parents.

Recurring patterns include:

pickup and drop-off coordination

dentist and school reminders

shopping lists

“did you remember” messages

last-minute childcare problems

check-ins with older kids

birthday logistics

family schedule reshuffling

sports practice timing

low-level household crisis management

Subtypes include the hyper-coordinator, the stressed parent, the newly-texting dad, the school-group organizer, the over-checker, and the generally competent but tired parent who runs life through short messages and calendar fragments.

Their traffic spikes in morning prep, mid-afternoon pickup windows, and early evening.

The Elderly (100 agents)

Grandparents, retirees, widows, widowers, older relatives, and socially steady older adults. Their tone is warmer, slower, and more complete. Many write in full sentences. Some sign off properly. Some send messages that feel like miniature letters.

Subtypes include:

the retired grandfather who texts formally and kindly

the digitally confident grandmother who sends article links

the one whose daughter often texts on their behalf

the lonely but lucid elder who replies late and at length

the habit-based checker-in

the gentle worrier who references the news

the practical appointment confirmer

the family historian who remembers everything

the affectionate sender of seasonal greetings

the occasional joke-forwarder

Their volume is low, but their language is highly distinctive and emotionally legible. They are most active in late morning and early evening.

The Sports Fans (100 agents)

Ordinary people whose communication changes completely around matches, tournaments, fantasy deadlines, and transfer rumors. Most of the time they are quiet. Then an event starts and their phones light up.

Subtypes include:

the fanatic who believes every referee decision is a crime

the fantasy obsessive who treats sport as spreadsheet input

the calm analyst who is occasionally right and never humble about it

the bandwagon fan who appears only in good times

the club loyalist

the national team only fan

the betting-adjacent stats person

the group chat instigator

the prediction spammer

the post-loss ghost

Their activity is highly event-driven, with sharp synchronized bursts tied to match start, halftime, late-game drama, and final whistle.

The Shoppers (100 agents)

People whose texts revolve around buying, waiting, comparing, returning, or recommending. This group produces constant low-stakes commercial chatter and a steady stream of links, screenshots, and tiny frustrations.

Subtypes include:

the bargain hunter with a spreadsheet

the impulsive buyer who cycles through regret and excitement

the parcel-delay veteran who remains strangely calm

the size-check asker

the resale app negotiator

the customs complainer

the group-order coordinator

the “is this a scam?” sender

the beauty/fashion recommender

the home-gear researcher

This group creates a realistic background hum across the week, especially lunchtime, evenings, and Sundays.

The Travelers (100 agents)

These agents are cyclical. For long periods, they are almost silent. Then a trip begins and their message rate spikes. Their traffic is structured by transit: departures, gate changes, hotel check-ins, delays, location updates, recommendations, and arrival confirmations.

Subtypes include:

frequent flyers who text almost like airline staff

enthusiastic holiday travelers who narrate everything

unlucky travelers with constant disruptions

business travelers coordinating timings

backpackers asking for tips

city-break planners

family trip coordinators

airport complainers

itinerary sharers

the person who always texts “boarding now”

Their message spikes are easy to identify because the language shifts toward time, movement, arrivals, and temporary locations.

The Gamers (100 agents)

Mostly quiet during the day, highly active at night. Their texts are coordination-heavy and socially compressed: who is on, who is late, who disconnected, what changed in the patch, who clipped something funny, who is carrying, who is throwing.

Subtypes include:

the raid leader

the unstable internet apologiser

the eager beginner

the patch complainer

the tournament grinder

the late-night duo queue regular

the all-caps celebrator

the silent but reliable joiner

the clip sender

the friend who says “5 min” and appears 40 minutes later

This group has strong 19:00 to 02:00 activity patterns and dense repeated contact graphs.

The Health People (100 agents)

Routine-driven, upbeat, and repetitive in a useful way. They message about workouts, nutrition, runs, recovery, sleep, class times, gym partners, small milestones, and occasional setbacks.

Subtypes include:

the marathon trainer

the casual gym person who needs accountability

the meal-prep evangelist

the step-count competitor

the early-morning runner

the Sunday reset planner

the supplement asker

the class-booking coordinator

the injury complainer

the quietly disciplined person who just keeps going

Their patterns are notably consistent across weeks, with spikes in early morning, after work, and Sunday planning windows.

The General Everyday Crowd (100 agents)

To make the network feel more real at scale, there is also a mixed category of ordinary people who do not fit neatly into a single lifestyle bucket but create essential background realism.

This group includes:

students in their twenties

part-time workers

unemployed people job-searching

neighbors

cousins

old school friends

casual daters

freelancers

service workers with irregular shifts

people whose texting style is mostly reactive rather than proactive

They help prevent the network from looking too cleanly segmented. Their patterns are messy, human, and overlapping.

The Bad Actors (100 agents)

These 100 agents are not one giant obvious block. That would be too easy to detect and too unrealistic. Instead, they are organized into a layered ecosystem of small tightly connected covert clusters, plus a handful of bridges, facilitators, and semi-legitimate fronts.

From the outside, many of them look ordinary. They have harmless side conversations, routine contacts, and plausible everyday traffic. What makes them different is not any single message. It is the pattern:

tighter recipient circles

repeated sender-receiver loops

unusual timing consistency

more deliberate silence

bursts of activity around narrow windows

language that avoids specifics while still coordinating action

fewer truly casual messages inside the group

subtle cross-linking through a small number of connector agents

To keep this safe and simulation-friendly, think of them as a coordinated covert network rather than writing them as an operational blueprint.

Structure of the 100 bad actors
1. Core Cells (60 agents)

These are divided into six cells of ten. Each cell is tightly knit, mutually familiar, and behaviorally distinct from the background population. Within a given cell, there are role-like differences:

one directional figure who sets pace

one or two movement/coordination contacts

one finance-adjacent contact

one logistics/problem-solving contact

one watcher or reporter

one recruiter or relationship-builder

one technical hygiene person

one stock/inventory/timing person

one front-facing ordinary-looking contact

These cells do not speak in clear terms. They rely on shorthand, assumed context, vague references, and consistent internal phrasing. Their wording is not dramatic. It is flat, efficient, and slightly evasive.

2. Bridge Actors (15 agents)

These agents connect cells that otherwise would not talk directly. They do not have the highest volume, but they are structurally important. They create sparse but crucial overlaps between groups. Without them, the covert network fragments into isolated islands.

They often look unremarkable in content. Their anomaly is graph-based.

3. Front-Facing Operators (10 agents)

These maintain highly plausible normal traffic: small businesses, hospitality, transport, casual buying and selling, side hustles, repairs, delivery-like chatter, or admin-like conversations. Most of what they text really is ordinary. That is what makes them effective covers in the simulation.

4. Peripheral Recruits and Test Contacts (10 agents)

These are the edge-of-network people. Some are uncertain. Some are opportunistic. Some drift in and out. Their conversations are more ambiguous, more exploratory, and less dense than the core cells. They create realistic gray-zone traffic.

5. Dormant or Low-Volume Specialists (5 agents)

These message infrequently, but when they do, others respond quickly. Their behavioral signature is low volume, high consequence. They help create the pattern where importance does not equal frequency.

Network Behavior at 1,000-Bot Scale

At this size, the scenario starts to feel like an actual communications environment rather than a toy example.

What the normal population looks like

The 900 ordinary agents generate:

broad variation in language style

natural repetition of contacts

circadian rhythms

weekday vs weekend differences

event-based spikes

family clusters

friend clusters

hobby clusters

work clusters

uneven reply delays

forgotten threads

overlapping communities

Most people live in small social neighborhoods of 5 to 25 meaningful contacts, with a few heavier ties and many lighter ones.

What the covert population looks like

The 100 bad actors do not dominate volume, but they stand out in structure:

higher internal density

lower randomness in whom they contact

more synchronized windows

fewer emotionally empty or playful exchanges inside the group

more deliberate handoff-style patterns

more short acknowledgments and less genuine social drift

bridge nodes with high betweenness

front-facing contacts whose normal and abnormal traffic sit unusually close together

Temporal Dynamics

To make the 1,000-bot simulation feel alive, the full network should breathe over time.

Daily rhythm

06:00–09:00: parents, commuters, runners, elderly check-ins

09:00–15:00: office workers, service coordination, low teen activity

15:00–18:00: teenagers spike, parents coordinate, shoppers browse

18:00–22:00: family logistics, gaming coordination, sports chatter, health routines

22:00–02:00: teenagers, gamers, oversharers, occasional covert bursts

02:00–06:00: very low baseline, with rare but suspicious concentrated traffic

Weekly rhythm

weekdays are driven by work, school, and logistics

Fridays and Saturdays produce more teenage, social, travel, and sports traffic

Sundays produce planning, recovery, shopping, family, and health messages

covert clusters may show narrower repeated windows that do not fully match normal lifestyle patterns

Event rhythm

The network should spike around:

football matches

school events

commute disruptions

holiday travel periods

online game updates

parcel delivery waves

weather shifts

paydays

month-end office stress

occasional anomalous coordinated windows in covert cells

Why this works as a 1,000-bot scenario

This version scales well because it has:

enough ordinary texture to feel real

enough subgroup diversity to avoid repetition

enough overlap to produce believable graph structure

enough anomalous coordination to test detection systems

enough timing variation to support temporal analysis

enough role depth to generate synthetic conversations without everyone sounding the same

The key idea is that the normal world should be noisy, repetitive, affectionate, annoying, boring, funny, and inconsistent. The covert world should not be “movie villain” obvious. It should feel quieter, narrower, more deliberate, and slightly wrong in aggregate.
